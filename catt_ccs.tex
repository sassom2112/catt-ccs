Adversarial evasion evaluations of Network Intrusion Detection Systems
(NIDS) consistently overstate attack success because they omit domain
constraint projection from the inner loop of gradient-based attacks.
Our prior work (CATT, AISec '26) quantified this gap in white-box
settings against a single MLP architecture.  In this paper we ask:
\emph{does the constraint inflation gap persist when attacks transfer to
the classifiers practitioners actually deploy?}

We evaluate constrained and unconstrained FGSM and PGD attacks
against three classifier families---MLP (white-box surrogate),
Random Forest, and XGBoost---across three benchmark datasets
(UNSW-NB15, CICIDS2017, NSL-KDD) with three random seeds each.

We further show that constrained PGD is the optimal adaptive attack
against the ConstraintSpec projection layer, closing a potential
gap between our evaluation and the adaptive adversary model required
by the CCS ML Security track.

\section{Threat Model}
\label{sec:threat}

We consider an adversary who seeks to evade a deployed NIDS classifier
by crafting malicious network flows that are misclassified as benign.
We formalize the threat model along four dimensions required by the
CCS ML Security track.

\subsection{Attacker}

\textbf{White-box (surrogate).}
The adversary has full access to a surrogate model---weights,
architecture, and preprocessing pipeline---trained on a representative
subset of the same traffic distribution as the target.  This models an
adversary who has reverse-engineered or independently trained a
functionally equivalent model.

\textbf{Black-box (transfer).}
The adversary generates adversarial examples against the surrogate and
submits them to an unknown target classifier.  The adversary receives
only binary decisions (attack/benign) from the target.  This is the
realistic model for adversaries who do not have access to the deployed
classifier's internals.

\subsection{Threat Surface}

Attacks perturb the \emph{preprocessed feature vector} presented to the
classifier---the 40-dimensional StandardScaler-normalized representation
for UNSW-NB15, the 78-dimensional representation for CICIDS2017, and the
38-dimensional representation for NSL-KDD.  We explicitly model the
preprocessing pipeline as part of the attack surface: constraint bounds
are computed in the transformed space via
$\ell_i = (\text{lo}_i - \mu_i)/\sigma_i$,
$u_i = (\text{hi}_i - \mu_i)/\sigma_i$.
Perturbations to categorical (one-hot) features are excluded; only
numeric features are perturbed, reflecting that categorical fields such
as protocol type are generally immutable at the network level.

\subsection{Generality}

We demonstrate that constraint inflation is not an artifact of a single
architecture.  All attacks are generated against the MLP surrogate and
evaluated against MLP (white-box), Random Forest (transfer), and
XGBoost (transfer).  RF and XGBoost are the dominant classifiers in
deployed NIDS~\cite{he2023survey}; if the gap collapses under transfer,
the AISec result would be an MLP-specific phenomenon.

\subsection{Practicality}

Perturbation budgets are expressed in both StandardScaler-normalized
space and original-domain units (Table~\ref{tab:calibration}).  We show
that $\varepsilon = 0.20$---the most common benchmark setting in prior
work---corresponds to [PLACEHOLDER: X packets / Y bytes / Z ms] on
UNSW-NB15, a perturbation that is [feasible / non-trivial] to achieve
through selective traffic shaping.

\subsection{Adaptive Adversary}

The strongest adaptive adversary against our ConstraintSpec projection
is one who exploits the projection geometry to find adversarial examples
that survive clamping.  We show that \emph{constrained PGD is precisely
this adaptive attack}: at each step, the adversary moves in the sign of
the gradient and then projects back onto both the $\varepsilon$-ball and
the valid feature manifold.  No strictly stronger adaptive attack exists
within the $L_\infty + \text{ConstraintSpec}$ threat model, because
the projection is a closed-form clamp that the optimizer already
accounts for through the inner-loop application.  This means our
constrained evasion rates are \emph{upper bounds} on what an optimal
adaptive adversary can achieve, not lower bounds.

\section{Adaptive Adversary Proof Sketch}
\label{app:adaptive}

\textbf{Claim.} Constrained PGD (Algorithm~1 in the main paper) is the
strongest $L_\infty$-bounded adaptive adversary against the
ConstraintSpec projection layer.

\textbf{Proof sketch.}  Let $\Pi_\mathcal{P}$ be the ConstraintSpec
projection (element-wise clamp to $[\ell_i, u_i]$) and
$\Pi_\varepsilon$ be the $L_\infty$ ball projection (clamp to
$[x_0 - \varepsilon, x_0 + \varepsilon]$).  Both projections are
non-expansive and applied in closed form.  At each step, the adversary
solves:
\[
  x^{(t+1)} = \Pi_\varepsilon\!\left(\Pi_\mathcal{P}\!\left(
    x^{(t)} + \alpha \cdot \operatorname{sign}\!\left(
      \nabla_x \mathcal{L}(\theta, x^{(t)}, y)
    \right)
  \right)\right).
\]
An adversary who knows $\Pi_\mathcal{P}$ and wants to maximise loss
subject to $\Pi_\varepsilon \circ \Pi_\mathcal{P}$ cannot do better
than taking the gradient step and then projecting, because the gradient
of the \emph{projected} loss $\mathcal{L}(\theta, \Pi_\mathcal{P}(x), y)$
with respect to the \emph{unprojected} $x$ is zero for features that
hit their bounds (the gradient is blocked by the clamp's subgradient of
zero at the boundary).  Constrained PGD is therefore a fixed point of
the best-response problem under this threat model. $\square$
